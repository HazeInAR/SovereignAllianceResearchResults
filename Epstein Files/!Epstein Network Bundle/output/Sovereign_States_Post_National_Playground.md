## The hypocrisy finale: anti-government engineer turned vendor
Thiel’s entire pitch has been that federal agencies are “deeply sclerotic and deeply nonfunctioning” and that bureaucrats—from the Fed to the Energy Department—have overstepped, such as the Solyndra debacle he cites as proof they can’t be trusted. ## Beyond politics
In his 2009 essay “The Education of a Libertarian,” Thiel admits he “no longer believe[s] that freedom and democracy are compatible” and that the draining grind of electoral politics only beats the intelligent few into disillusionment. The only escape, he argues, is to “focus efforts on new technologies that may create a new space for freedom,” because “there are no truly free places left in our world.” He explicitly sketches three fronts: cyberspace (PayPal’s new currency, Facebook-style communities), outer space (a libertarian frontier once rocketry improves), and new technologies more broadly. Those fronts are the operating system for Epstein’s and Thiel’s post-national play: democracy gets written off as a dead end, so you retreat into secret currencies, private ceremonies, techno-feudal intelligence nets—and seed those escapes with crypto cities, Vatican stagecraft, and Palantir’s surveillance stack.

Thiel also explicitly mourns the end of the 1920s as the last decade when American politics could still “feel optimistic” for libertarians. The intervening expansions of welfare beneficiaries and women’s suffrage, he says, have rendered “capitalist democracy” into an oxymoron because those new constituencies stiffen the political system with demands for redistribution. That’s a feminist-adjacent dog whistle: the more rights (and influence) women gain, the more the masses push against elite freedoms, which is why Thiel’s solution is to create offshore, techno-feudal enclaves outside any democratic reach.
