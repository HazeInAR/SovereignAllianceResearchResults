# Current Events: Epstein’s Post-Conviction Bank
**Source:** https://www.nytimes.com/2020/02/04/business/jeffrey-epstein-estate-bank.html
**Published:** February 4, 2020 (NYT)

## Summary
- After Epstein became a registered sex offender, he pivoted from money management to tech/algorithm work and in 2014 secured an unusual international banking license in the U.S. Virgin Islands for Southern Country International.
- The territory had passed its international banking entity law just a year earlier to attract offshore investment, modeled on Puerto Rico’s regime. These banks offer offshore investors favorable taxes while the territory expects locals to manage the entity, and regulators have warned they’re high-risk channels for money laundering. The NY Fed even temporarily halted Fed services for such banks while stricter rules were drafted.
- Epstein’s application was evasive about his criminal record: he acknowledged a guilty plea in Florida but described it as “a relatively brief period” and said the federal investigation was “discontinued.” He did not disclose his nonprosecution agreement with federal prosecutors, which Richard Carnell (former Treasury assistant secretary for financial institutions) said would outrage regulators because it concealed the wider list of potential underage victims tied to the plea.
- In the application, Epstein listed James E. Staley (then-CEO of Barclays) and Andrew Farkas (New York real estate tycoon who co-owned St. Thomas property with Epstein) as references, along with JPMorgan and FirstBank, neither of whom said they knew they were on the paperwork.
- The bank, approved despite his conviction, was licensed to serve offshore clients. Epstein apparently never marketed it or hired staff; regulators noted the license carried limited oversight and that the bank never seemed to do business.
- Court documents show Southern Country held $693,157 in assets at Epstein’s death. In mid-December the estate sent $15.5 million in two checks to the bank, which returned $2.6 million, leaving the net receipt at $12.9 million. By year-end the bank’s assets were listed as $499,759 and the filings do not explain how the money was used.
- The estate told officials it would not renew the bank’s license. Around the time the license was granted, the territory also approved a tax break for Southern Trust, which was said to be mining DNA/financial data; the tax holiday gave the company an effective 3.9% rate on $300 million profit and was signed by Governor John de Jongh Jr., whose wife worked for Epstein.
- Southern Country has become part of a Virgin Islands lawsuit. Attorney General Denise George accused Epstein of sullying the territory, asked to seize his islands, dissolve shell companies, and intervene in the estate administration so assets can be secured for victims. She cited potential conflicts of interest because coexecutors Darren Indyke and Richard Kahn served as officers in Epstein companies, including Southern Country and Southern Trust. Judge Hermon-Purcell heard arguments and said she’d rule later.
- Following Epstein’s death in 2019, more than $12 million from the estate was transferred to Southern Country International, despite the bank’s long-dormant status. At a Virgin Islands estate hearing, Magistrate Judge Carolyn Hermon-Purcell pressed the estate attorneys for an explanation, noting the transfers stood out among routine payments and asking for a fuller accounting.
- Estate lawyers claimed some payments were errors, but the judge remained unsatisfied and ordered follow-up. The transfers were documented alongside routine bills (cable, phone) even though the bank had no apparent business.
