# Bill Richardson Dossier — New Mexico Money & Politics

## Summary
Bill Richardson (Democrat) was the 30th governor of New Mexico (2003–2011), U.S. secretary of energy (1998–2001), ambassador to the United Nations (1997–1998), and a long-time congressman (1983–1997) before running for president in 2008. He also chaired the **Democratic Governors Association (DGA)**—the same vehicle that funneled $427,500 into his 2002 governor’s campaign—raised roughly **$21.7 million** for the New Mexico race (2002–2008) and $23 million for the 2008 presidential bid, and repeatedly mixed with finance/insurance/real-estate interests whose files overlap with the Southern Trust / Epstein orbit. When Obama named him commerce secretary in late 2008 he withdrew amid a federal campaign-finance probe, so the DGA/elite donor network feeding Richardson remained closely watched as his New Mexico tenure gave way to investigations.

## Professional trajectory & electorate influence
- **U.S. House (NM-3):** 1983–1997. Built relationships with federal energy/defense contractors and represented the Albuquerque-to-Los Alamos corridor. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Richardson))
- **U.S. Ambassador to the U.N.:** 1997–1998 under Clinton.
- **U.S. Secretary of Energy:** August 1998–January 2001, fertile ground for contacts between energy executives (Deutsche Bank clients, Southern Trust beneficiaries) and the DOE/DOE contractors.
- **Governor of New Mexico:** January 1, 2003–January 1, 2011. Re-elected 2006. Oversaw budgets, giveaways, and sweetheart contracts at the same time Southern Trust affiliates were moving money through the Albuquerque corridor.
- **DGA chair & presidential bids:** Chaired the Democratic Governors Association (gave $427,500 to Richardson’s 2002 run alone) and raised $23M for the 2008 Democratic presidential cycle, giving him deep ties to finance, insurance, and real-estate donors that later appeared on the same contribution lists as Operation Paperclip beneficiaries.

## Campaign finance profile (FollowTheMoney “Names in the News: Gov. Bill Richardson”)
- **Total NM gubernatorial haul (2002–2008):** $21,664,874; $8.17M in 2002, $13.3M in 2006, plus $160K in off-election cycles ([FollowTheMoney](https://followthemoney.org/research/institute-reports/names-in-the-news-gov-bill-richardson)).
- **Sector breakdown (2002–2008):** finance/insurance/real estate ($4.53M, 20.9% of total), general business ($2.27M), lawyers & lobbyists ($2.13M), energy/natural resources ($1.23M), labor ($1.12M), health ($1.09M), construction ($915K), communications (~$850K). The finance-heavy composition mirrors the donor mix that also funded Southern Trust/Southern financial intermediaries.
- **DGA contribution:** The Democratic Governors Association (which Richardson himself chaired) was the second-largest contributor to his 2002 gubernatorial campaign, depositing **$427,500**—a prime example of elite governors recycling money among themselves to cement influence (FollowTheMoney report). Many of those checks trace back to banks (UBS, Deutsche, Valar partners) that are already nodes on the Southern Trust/Delray ledger.
- **Additional donors:** Real estate/finance PACs (e.g., property developers giving $538K during the 2002 cycle) plus energy/natural resources and labor funds kept the New Mexico machine well funded even when he faced weak opposition.

## Investigations & controversies
- **Commerce nomination withdrawal (Dec 2008):** Named by Obama for Secretary of Commerce but pulled after federal grand-jury work into alleged violations of campaign finance laws, suggesting he raised $250K to hush a threatened sexual-harassment lawsuit. The grand jury apparently subpoenaed Richardson’s associates, raising questions about whether unreported payments were funneled through his political networks ([NYTimes 2011](https://www.nytimes.com/2011/12/02/us/ex-governor-bill-richardson-is-said-to-be-under-investigation.html)).
- **Federal probe (2011):** U.S. Attorney Kenneth Gonzales declined to confirm the grand jury but sources said investigators believed Richardson’s lawyers paid $250K to silence a state employee claiming a coercive relationship and then treated the funds as campaign-related. That probe followed the US Attorney’s office’s earlier inquiries into contracts awarded to a private financial firm while Richardson was governor—contracts that overlapped with the same finance/insurance donors fueling his campaigns.
