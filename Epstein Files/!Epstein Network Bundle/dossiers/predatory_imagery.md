# Predatory Imagery in Hollywood & Luxury Fashion

This dossier catalogs the stylized use of Datura, Brugmansia, Morning Glory, Blue Lotus, and related entheogenic flora within fashion, fragrance, and editorial campaigns. These plants appear as **visual codes** for danger, euphoria, hypnotic control, and Luciferian duality—aligning with the propaganda/psychological-prep thesis laid out in the Real Baron context files.

## Key fashion house campaigns (2010s–2026)
- **Gucci** – "Utopian Fantasy" (Spring/Summer 2018) and the 2024–2026 Flora fragrance line (Miley Cyrus) both integrate blue Morning Glory vines and narcotic floral palettes to suggest altered perception. Digital painter Ignasi Monreal and the Pierre et Gilles duo are credited in the source note (`.openclaw/media/inbound/68288834-fd41-4c42-a379-a07192e143aa.txt`).
- **Alexander McQueen** – "Angels and Demons" (Fall/Winter 2010) and the Sarah Burton-era botanical prints (2023–2024) highlight Brugmansia trumpets as divine-but-deadly sculptures, reinforcing the "beautiful danger" motif.
- **Dior** – "Flower Men" (Spring 2024) and the Dior Poison lineage (1985–2010s) lean into hallucinogenic pastels and venomous vines, signalling risk through color saturation and ominous silhouettes.
- **Prada** – FW23 featuring Massive Attack’s "Angel" soundtrack centers large, stark trumpet blossoms, echoing Datura’s minimalist yet threatening geometry.

## Photographers / creative directors who shaped the "botanical narcotic" look
- **Nick Knight** – "Flora" project and the Plato's Atlantis collection visuals (2009/2010) stress flowers until they bleed, imitating hallucinogenic trails.
- **Steven Meisel** – Vogue Italia shoots like "State of Mind" (2007) and Dolce & Gabbana campaigns that used pale trumpet blossoms create a narcotic, medicated atmosphere.
- **Tim Walker** – Oversized Morning Glories and Angel’s Trumpets place models inside surreal, poisonous dreamscapes.
- **Paolo Roversi** – 8x10 Polaroids for Dior and Yohji Yamamoto blur edges, giving the Morning Glory a ghostly, otherworldly glow.
- **Guy Bourdin** – Early 1980s work staged Morning Glories as predatory vines that physically frame models, heightening psychological unease.

## Historical/visual references & pop-culture diffusion
- **1960s–1990s** – Morning Glories symbolized psychedelic liberation (Granny Takes a Trip, Summer of Love posters) while later high-fashion (Kenzo, Yves Saint Laurent Opium, Calvin Klein) pivoted to Heroin Chic and "beautiful death" depictions, often pairing with washed-out lighting and lethargic models.
- **Blue Lotus (Nymphaea caerulea)** – Used by Gucci Beauty, Bvlgari, and Prada Infusion lines to evoke ancient Egyptian trance states and rebirth symbology.
- **Hawaiian Baby Woodrose & Related Species** – Referenced in underground "legal high" forums and culty literature; used as a web of botanical legitimacy for the luxury aesthetic through LSA-related mystique.
- **Morning Glory/Blue Lotus in pop culture** – Oasis album, Pink Floyd "The Wall" animations, film titles, and wellness marketing that recast these plants as dreamy yet dangerous.
